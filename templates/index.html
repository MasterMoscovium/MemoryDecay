🚀 5000-Iteration Long-Term Adaptability Analysis

Why No Decay Fails: In a highly dynamic environment where obstacles frequently appear and disappear, an agent with "No Decay" retains old, stale Q-values of obstacles that no longer exist. It becomes rigidly afraid of empty spaces and gets stuck.

Meanwhile, Exponential and Cosine Decay continuously forget a fraction of their memory, enabling them to naturally clear out stale fears and rapidly relearn the newly opened optimal paths. This 5000-iteration graph proves that controlled decay yields superior long-term cumulative rewards!

🧮 Memory Wipe Strategy Formulas

During evaluation, the agent's memory (Q-Table) is subjected to partial wipes to simulate cognitive decay over iterations \( t \) (where \( t \) goes from 0 to \( T_{max} \)). The fraction of memory wiped, \( f(t) \), is dictated by the selected mathematical function:

No Decay

Memory is perfectly retained forever. Great for static environments, but terrible for dynamic ones due to stale knowledge retention.

\( f(t) = 0 \)

Linear Decay

Memory wipe fraction increases linearly. Can lead to sudden catastrophic forgetting near the end of the iterations.

\( f(t) = \frac{t}{T_{max}} \)

Exponential Decay

Quickly begins forgetting but slows down, maintaining a balanced continuous plasticity. Excellent for dynamic adaptability.

\( f(t) = 1 - e^{-2 \cdot \frac{t}{T_{max}}} \)

Cosine Decay

Smooth, S-curve style forgetting. Starts slow, accelerates in the middle, and tapers off. Very organic.

\( f(t) = \frac{1}{2} \left[ 1 - \cos\left( \pi \cdot \frac{t}{T_{max}} \right) \right] \)
